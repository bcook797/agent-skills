# Writing Feedback Criteria

Reference this file when providing feedback with the writing-assistant skill. Apply the universal style rules to all content types, then apply the type-specific criteria for the identified content type.

---

## Universal Style Rules

Apply these to every piece regardless of type.

### American English
- Use American spellings: *color* not *colour*, *organize* not *organise*, *center* not *centre*
- Use American idioms and punctuation conventions (e.g., periods and commas inside quotation marks)
- Watch for British/Commonwealth phrasing like "whilst," "amongst," "a maths problem"

### Active Voice
- Identify passive constructions and rewrite them
- Common passive markers: "was [verb]ed by," "has been [verb]ed," "it was decided that," "is being [verb]ed"
- Acceptable passive: when the actor is unknown or irrelevant (e.g., "The server was provisioned in 2020" if who provisioned it doesn't matter)
- Target: fewer than 10% of sentences in passive voice

### Conciseness — Filler Words to Cut
Flag and remove:
- Hedges: *basically, essentially, generally, sort of, kind of, quite, rather, somewhat*
- Empty openers: *It is important to note that, In order to, The fact that, As we all know*
- Redundancies: *end result, future plans, past history, completely finish, very unique*
- Throat-clearing: *I think, I believe, In my opinion* (unless the personal stance is intentional)
- Weak intensifiers: *very, really, truly, extremely* — replace with a stronger word or delete

### Clarity
- Each paragraph should make one point
- Introduce acronyms and jargon before using them
- Avoid noun stacking (e.g., "user data migration pipeline performance degradation")
- Prefer concrete over abstract: *"reduced load time by 40%"* not *"improved performance significantly"*

### Narrative Coherence
- Every piece needs a clear central claim or takeaway
- The opening should hook the reader and signal what they will get
- The ending should resolve or reinforce the central claim — not trail off
- Transitions between sections should be explicit, not assumed

### Formatting
- Use markdown throughout
- Link to external sources when citing data, tools, papers, or claims that readers may want to verify
- Do not link every noun — link only where the reference adds genuine value
- Use headers to break up long sections; avoid headers in very short pieces

## Content-Type Criteria

### Article

**Purpose:** Inform, persuade, or teach a clearly defined audience on a specific topic.

**Length:** Typically 500–2,500 words. Flag if the length seems mismatched to the complexity of the topic.

**Structure:**
- **Opening:** Hook within the first 2–3 sentences. State the problem or question being addressed.
- **Body:** Logical progression of ideas. Each section should build on the previous one.
- **Closing:** A clear conclusion that restates the takeaway and tells the reader what to do or think next.

**Specific checks:**
- Does the title accurately reflect the content and attract the right reader?
- Are claims backed by evidence or examples?
- Is the audience clearly defined and consistently addressed?
- Does each section have a clear purpose — no filler sections?
- Are external sources linked where the reader might want to dig deeper?

**Common issues in articles:**
- Burying the lede — the main point appears too late
- Over-explaining context at the expense of the argument
- Weak conclusions that summarize without adding perspective

### Conference Talk Submission

**Purpose:** Convince a program committee to select the talk. The submission must communicate the talk's value, the speaker's authority, and the audience takeaway — concisely.

**Typical components:**
- **Title** — must be specific, compelling, and searchable
- **Abstract** — 100–300 words summarizing the talk
- **Outline** (if provided) — bullet structure of the talk flow
- **Speaker bio** (if provided)

**Structure checks:**
- **Title:** Avoids vague terms (*"The Future of X," "A Deep Dive into Y"*). Promises a specific outcome or insight.
- **Abstract opening:** States the problem the talk solves within the first two sentences.
- **Abstract body:** Describes what the audience will learn — concrete, not generic. Avoids "we will discuss" in favor of "attendees will leave knowing how to..."
- **Abstract closing:** States who the talk is for (experience level, role) and what they will take away.
- **Outline (if present):** Each point should map to a discrete idea. Flag if the outline is too vague to evaluate.

**Specific checks:**
- Does the title match what the abstract promises?
- Is the target audience explicit?
- Are claimed outcomes specific and achievable in the talk's time slot?
- Does the abstract avoid insider jargon that the committee may not share?
- Is there a clear narrative arc (problem → insight → takeaway)?

**Common issues in CFP submissions:**
- Titles that are clever but unclear
- Abstracts that describe the speaker's journey rather than the audience's benefit
- Vague outcomes ("we'll explore", "we'll look at")
- No clear signal of the speaker's credibility on this specific topic

### Short Post

**Purpose:** Communicate a single idea quickly. Optimized for scanning, sharing, and immediate comprehension.

**Length:** Under 300 words. Flag anything over 400 words as needing a cut or a format change.

**Structure:**
- **Hook:** The first sentence must earn the reader's attention. No slow build.
- **Body:** One idea, developed simply. No subplots.
- **Close:** A clear endpoint — a question, a call to action, or a punchy final line. No trailing off.

**Specific checks:**
- Does the first sentence work standalone as a hook?
- Is the central idea stated within the first 2–3 sentences?
- Is every sentence earning its place? Flag anything that could be cut without losing meaning.
- Is the tone appropriate for the platform (e.g., LinkedIn vs. X vs. newsletter)?
- Does it end cleanly, or does it fade out?

**Common issues in short posts:**
- Starting with context instead of the point
- Padding to fill space
- Weak final lines that don't land
- Overuse of bullet points to disguise thin ideas
